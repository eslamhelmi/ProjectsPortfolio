Time Series Decomposition & Promotional Impact Analysis

IIF Certified Project | Lancaster University CMAF Program — Task 1

Overview

This project focused on analyzing daily sales data to understand underlying patterns and quantify the impact of promotional activities. Using time series decomposition and the ADAM framework with external regressors, I identified trend, seasonality, and promotion-driven effects, providing actionable business recommendations.

Project Scope

  • 70 days of daily sales data
  • Two promotional activities (Promo1, Promo2)
  • Weekly seasonality pattern
  • 14-day forecast horizon

Tools & Methods

  • R (forecast, smooth packages)
  • Time series decomposition
  • ETS(M,A,A) model
  • ADAM with external regressors

1. Data Exploration

Initial visualization of the sales data revealed clear patterns:

Key Observations

  • Strong weekly seasonality: Clear repeating pattern every 7 days
  • Upward trend: Sales gradually increasing over the 10-week period
  • Constant amplitude: Seasonal swings remain roughly the same size, suggesting an additive model
  • Promotional effects: Promo1 (red markers) appears on both peaks and troughs; Promo2 (blue) mostly coincides with low-sales days

2. Time Series Decomposition

The sales data was decomposed into trend, seasonal, and irregular components using additive decomposition:

Trend Component

The trend starts at approximately 98 units and increases to about 156 by the end of the period — roughly a 60% increase over 10 weeks. This indicates strong underlying growth in demand.

Seasonal Component

3. Residual Analysis

The decomposition achieved an RMSE of 16.81, indicating reasonably small residuals. However, examination of the irregular component revealed important patterns:

Figure 3: Irregular (residual) component with promotional activity indicators

Residual Statistics

  • Min: -32.3 | Max: +62.1
  • Mean: 0.0 | Median: -1.05
  • Residuals centered around zero but slightly right-skewed
  • Notable spikes (+40 to +60) in weeks 6, 7, and 9 — coinciding with Promo1 activities

4. Promotional Impact Analysis

To quantify promotional effects, I filtered for observations where promotions occurred AND residuals exceeded ±20:

Date Sales Promo1 Promo2 Residual
2024-01-13 48 1 1 -29.3
2024-02-01 184 1 0 +36.7
2024-02-08 164 0 1 +22.4
2024-02-12 90 1 1 -22.2
2024-02-13 188 1 0 +62.1
2024-02-17 123 1 0 +23.7
2024-02-28 230 1 0 +57.7

Key Finding: When Promo1 runs alone, it consistently produces large positive residuals (+23 to +62). When both promotions run together, residuals are negative (-22 to -29), suggesting a cannibalization effect.

5. Quantifying Effects with ADAM

To precisely quantify promotional impacts, I used the ADAM framework (ETS with external regressors), modeling the interaction between promotions:

adam_model <- adam(data = mydata,
                   model = "MAA",
                   formula = y ~ Promo1 * Promo2,
                   lags = 7,
                   regressors = "use")

ADAM Model Results

Parameter Estimate Interpretation
Promo1 +36.3% Significant positive impact when run alone
Promo2 +15.7% Moderate positive impact when run alone
Promo1 × Promo2 -75.8% Strong negative interaction (cannibalization)

Model Fit: AICc = 558.4 vs. 721.5 for standard ETS — significantly better fit when promotions are modeled explicitly.

Interesting Finding: Beta (trend) dropped to zero in the ADAM model, suggesting the apparent trend in the base ETS was partially driven by promotional effects, not underlying growth.

6. Forecasting Methods Comparison

I compared four forecasting approaches for a 14-day horizon:

Method Captures Trend? Captures Seasonality? Captures Promotions?
Seasonal Naïve No Yes No
Moving Average (k=7) No No* No
SES (α=0.086) No No No
ETS(M,A,A) Yes Yes No

*MA with k=7 smooths out seasonality by averaging exactly one week.

Conclusion: ETS(M,A,A) is the most appropriate method as it captures both trend and seasonality. However, for operational forecasting, the ADAM model with promotional regressors provides superior accuracy when promotion schedules are known.

7. Business Recommendations

Continue Promo1

Promo1 demonstrates statistically significant and economically meaningful uplift (+36%). It should remain the primary promotional lever.

Revise Promo2

Promo2's impact (+16%) is weaker and it tends to run on low-sales days. Investigate why it's less effective and consider repositioning.

Avoid Overlap

Running both promotions together creates cannibalization (-76% net effect). Never run both simultaneously.

Key Learnings

  • Decomposition reveals hidden patterns: Without decomposition, promotional effects would be confounded with seasonality.
  • Residual analysis is powerful: Large residuals aren't just noise — they often indicate external drivers like promotions.
  • Interaction effects matter: The cannibalization effect would be missed without modeling the Promo1 × Promo2 interaction.
  • Apparent trends can be misleading: What looked like a 60% trend was partially promotional lift.
